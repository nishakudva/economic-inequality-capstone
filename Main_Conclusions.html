Economic Inequality Team · Final Conclusions (Nisha Kudva) UCI Adult Census · US Census Bureau, 1994
Capstone Conclusion

Education opens the door. It does not make the room equal.

Our main conclusion

Who earns above $50K in 1994 America is shaped less by effort than by structure. Education raises everyone’s odds but at the same credential, a person’s sex and type of employer still move their chances of a high income by 20–30 percentage points. And a model that predicts income at 84% accuracy quietly reproduces that inequity, finding high-earning women at barely half the rate it finds men. The lesson: “just get more education” was never a sufficient answer to income inequality.

Based on 26,904 cleaned census records (82.6% of the original 32,561). Every figure below is reproducible from the project code.

THE THREE FINDINGS BEHIND THE CONCLUSION
20.2 pts

Sex gap

32.3% of men earn >$50K vs 12.1% of women, and the gap is widest, not narrowest, among the most educated.

2.5×

Workclass gap

Incorporated self-employed earn >$50K at 55.6% vs 22.4% for private-sector, a gap that survives holding education constant.

35% vs 55%

Model unfairness

The best model’s recall on true high earners: 35% for women, 55% for men. Accurate on average, unequal in practice.

01 / EDUCATION HELPS EVERYONE — BUT NOT EQUALLY

The gender gap widens with the degree

The intuitive expectation is that a degree levels the playing field. The data shows the opposite: at every education level men out-earn women, and the gap is largest for Bachelor’s and Master’s holders. Among Bachelor’s graduates, 50.4% of men earn >$50K versus 22.6% of women with the identical credential.

02 / WHERE YOU WORK IS ITS OWN DIVIDE

Workclass adds inequality on top of education

The research question was whether the odds of a high income differ by type of employer, and whether that difference is just a side-effect of who hires graduates. It is not. The workclass gap persists when we compare people with the same education: among Bachelor’s holders, incorporated self-employed reach 61.9% versus 42.0% for private-sector employees. Structure, ownership, sector, hours, shapes outcomes independently of credentials.

03 / THE MODEL INHERITS THE INEQUALITY

84% accurate, but not fair

Eleven models were compared; Gradient Boosting won (84.1% accuracy, F1 0.62). But accuracy is a trap under class imbalance, a model that never predicts >$50K still scores 74%. The fairness audit is the real verdict: the model correctly identifies only 35% of high-earning women versus 55% of high-earning men. Deployed to screen people for loans or insurance, it would under-recognise exactly the group already disadvantaged in the data.

A model that looks fair on averaged accuracy can still be unfair to a specific group. And because it was trained on 1994 data, a “fair” model here would still not be fair applied to people today.

What this means, and what it does not

What we can conclude. In 1994, income was strongly structured by factors beyond individual effort. Education mattered, but sex and employer type independently shifted the odds of a high income by double-digit margins that education did not erase.

  • For policy: education access alone cannot close income gaps; interventions must also address pay equity by sex and access to higher-paying sectors and ownership.
  • For machine learning: never judge a model on average accuracy alone, a per-group fairness check is essential before any real-world use.

What we cannot claim. This is a 1994 snapshot. It documents a historical pattern and should motivate current measurement, it cannot state today’s exact gaps, and it does not prove a single cause. It also omits people the Census undercounted: undocumented, informal, and some unemployed workers.
